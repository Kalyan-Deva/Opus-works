## Problem

MITRE's eCTF is a national embedded-security competition where teams design a
secure device and then attack one another's designs. The 2026 challenge centered
on a Hardware Security Module (HSM): firmware that must protect secrets even when
an attacker has physical access to the chip, can glitch its power, and can probe
its timing. I led Team NEU-1 (NeuSense) as Team Lead.

## Approach

I architected the full cryptographic stack on a bare-metal ARM Cortex-M0+:

- **Encryption** with AES-CBC-256 driven by the `AESADV` hardware peripheral,
  using DMA-driven crypto offload so bulk operations run in constant time rather
  than leaking secrets through data-dependent execution.
- **A three-tier key hierarchy** derived with HKDF —
  `ROOT_SEED` → `DEVICE_SEED` → `ENC_SEED` / `SESSION_SEED` — so a compromise at
  one layer does not expose the layers above it.
- **HMAC-SHA256** authentication on every protected message to guarantee
  integrity and origin.
- **A fail-closed permission model** plus **PIN rate limiting**, so the device
  denies access by default and resists brute force.

## Outcome

The design hardened the firmware against the three failure modes that matter
most for a physical device: secret extraction from flash, side-channel timing
leakage, and fault-injection (glitching). Building it end-to-end — from the key
schedule down to DMA timing — was a lesson in how cryptographic theory survives
contact with real silicon.

## Problem

Electric distribution networks fail in correlated, cascading ways — knocking out
the wrong node can take down a whole district. The question I set out to answer:
which parts of the Boston metro grid matter most, and how does the network behave
under both random failures and a targeted attack?

## Approach

I modeled the Boston Metropolitan Electric Power Distribution Network as an
eight-node graph with an adjacency matrix, then computed structural metrics to
locate weak points:

- **Degree, betweenness, and eigenvector centrality** to rank how important each
  node is to the network's connectivity.
- **Spectral radius** of the adjacency matrix as a measure of overall robustness.
- **Multi-Criteria Betweenness-based Risk Assessment (MBRA)** to rank critical
  nodes under targeted versus random failure scenarios.
- **Fault tree analysis** on the Downtown Distribution Substation to trace how
  component-level failures propagate into a district-wide outage.

## Outcome

The analysis identified the substations whose loss would do the most structural
damage and framed the findings inside the DHS Energy Sector cybersecurity
framework. The work was submitted to Prof. Themis A. Papageorge in April 2026.
It reinforced a core idea of infrastructure defense: you protect a network by
understanding its topology, not just its perimeter.

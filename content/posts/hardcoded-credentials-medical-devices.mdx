When I started this project, I expected to find a single bug. What I found instead was a small lesson in how medical devices fail — not loudly, not in a way any one person decided on, but slowly, structurally, across a decade of design choices that each made sense at the time. The device I studied was the BD Alaris 8015 PC Unit, the brains of a widely deployed clinical infusion pump system. My goal was never to drop an exploit. It was to map the full attack surface of a real, fielded clinical device the way an adversary would, and then to ask the harder question: why does a device like this stay vulnerable for years after the problems are known?

The research package I produced ended up larger than I planned: a term paper, a threat model, a Data Flow Diagram, a set of sanitized credential-configuration samples, and a presentation deck. This post is the narrative version of that work. It walks through two findings — a known sensitive-data-exposure vulnerability, `CVE-2016-9355`, and a separately identified hardcoded-credentials weakness that maps to `CWE-798` — and uses them as a lens on the deeper structural reasons that the Internet of Medical Things stays soft.

## Why medical devices are uniquely hard to secure

Before any of the technical detail, I want to establish the thing that makes this domain different from the rest of security. If you come from web security or cloud, your mental model is built around fast iteration: a vulnerability is disclosed, a patch ships within days, and the install base updates over a release cycle measured in weeks. Almost none of that holds for clinical devices, and understanding why is the whole point.

The first reason is *lifecycle length*. A laptop is replaced every three or four years. A hospital infusion pump is a capital asset with a service life that can stretch past a decade. A device designed in the early 2010s, running an embedded operating system and software stack frozen at that era, can still be running on a hospital floor today. The threat landscape of 2016 is not the threat landscape the device now lives in, but the device has not moved.

The second reason is *uptime and safety constraints*. A pump that is administering medication cannot simply reboot to apply a patch. There is a patient attached to it. The change-management process around clinical equipment is, correctly, extremely conservative — you do not push firmware to a device delivering a continuous infusion the way you push a browser update. Every change has to be validated against the possibility that it alters dosing behavior. That conservatism is a feature for patient safety and a liability for security.

The third reason is *the patching pipeline itself*. With a general-purpose computer, the vendor patches and you apply it. With a regulated medical device, a software change can require re-validation, and depending on the nature of the change, interaction with regulators. The manufacturer cannot unilaterally and instantly alter a cleared device in the field without considering the regulatory implications of that change. The result is that the gap between "vulnerability known" and "fix deployed across the install base" is not measured in weeks. It is measured in product cycles.

The fourth reason is *the regulatory context*, which cuts both ways. Frameworks exist — the U.S. FDA has issued pre-market and post-market guidance on device cybersecurity, and standards like `IEC 62443` and `IEC 80001` (the latter dealing with risk management for IT networks incorporating medical devices) give hospitals and manufacturers a vocabulary. But guidance that matured in the late 2010s and 2020s does not retroactively secure a device designed before it existed. A device cleared a decade ago was held to the security expectations of a decade ago.

Put those four properties together and you get the defining situation of medical-device security: a long-lived, safety-critical, hard-to-patch device, often running software whose security assumptions predate the network it now sits on. That is the environment in which both of my findings live, and it is why neither of them is surprising.

## The device and its data flows

The BD Alaris 8015 PC Unit is the controller module of a modular infusion system. In plain terms, it is the computer that the pump channels attach to — it handles the user interface, holds configuration, communicates with the hospital network, and coordinates the actual fluid-delivery modules. From a security standpoint, the interesting thing is that it is not an isolated appliance. It is a networked node in a clinical environment, and that network connectivity is exactly what turns a mechanical dosing device into something with an attack surface worth modeling.

To do that modeling honestly, I started where you should always start: by drawing the system as data flows rather than as a box. A Data Flow Diagram (DFD) forces you to stop thinking about the device as a single trusted object and instead describe it as a set of processes, data stores, external entities, and — crucially — the trust boundaries between them. The trust boundaries are where the security questions live. Every time data crosses one, you have to ask who is on the other side and whether you trust them.

Here is a sanitized, illustrative rendering of the DFD I built. It is deliberately generic; it describes the shape of the system, not any proprietary internals.

```text title="alaris-8015-dfd.txt" showLineNumbers
                +-----------------------------+
                |   External Entity:          |
                |   Clinician / Operator      |
                +--------------+--------------+
                               |
                  (1) UI input / config entry
                               |
        == TRUST BOUNDARY: physical access to device ==
                               |
                               v
                +-----------------------------+
                |   Process:                  |
                |   PC Unit Controller SW     |
                +----+-------------------+----+
                     |                   |
        (2) read/write config     (3) infusion commands
                     |                   |
                     v                   v
        +------------------+    +----------------------+
        | Data Store:      |    | Process:             |
        | Config + Creds   |    | Pump Channel Modules |
        | (XML on device)  |    | (fluid delivery)     |
        +------------------+    +----------------------+
                     |
        (4) status / data sync
                     |
        == TRUST BOUNDARY: hospital network segment ==
                     |
                     v
        +-------------------------------+
        | External Entity:              |
        | Server / Management System    |
        +-------------------------------+
```

The act of drawing this is where the findings start to announce themselves. Two boundaries matter most. The first is the *network boundary* between the device and the management or server systems it talks to — that is where data in transit can be observed by anyone with a position on the segment. The second is the *configuration data store* on the device itself, the place where settings and, as it turned out, credentials live. Once you have named those two boundaries, the two findings are almost predictable.

## Finding one: sensitive data exposure (`CVE-2016-9355`)

The first finding is a known, published vulnerability. `CVE-2016-9355` describes a sensitive-data-exposure issue affecting the BD Alaris 8015 PC Unit. The essence of this class of weakness is straightforward: information that ought to be protected is accessible to someone who should not have it, typically because it is transmitted or stored without adequate protection. In the DFD, this maps directly to data crossing the network trust boundary in a state where an observer on the segment could read it.

I want to be careful and accurate here rather than dramatic. I am referencing the NVD entry for the device's general data-exposure issue; I am not going to invent a CVSS score or attribute vendor statements I cannot verify. What matters for the threat model is the *category* of the weakness and where it sits in the data flow. Sensitive data exposure on a clinical device is dangerous for reasons specific to healthcare. The data moving through and around an infusion system can include clinically and operationally meaningful information, and in a regulated environment the unauthorized disclosure of patient-associated data is not just a security problem — it is a compliance and patient-privacy problem with its own legal weight under healthcare data-protection regimes.

The deeper point this finding illustrates is *positional risk*. The vulnerability does not require an attacker to break the device's logic. It requires an attacker to be in the right place on the network — and on a flat hospital network where clinical devices share a segment with general IT, the right place is not hard to reach. That is the recurring theme of IoMT: the device's own weaknesses are amplified by the network it is dropped into.

## Finding two: hardcoded credentials (`CWE-798`)

The second finding is the one I find more instructive, because it is not a single CVE so much as a design pattern that shows up everywhere in embedded systems. While working through the device's configuration handling, I identified a hardcoded-credentials weakness — credentials baked into the device's configuration rather than provisioned, rotated, or derived per-deployment. This maps to `CWE-798`, the MITRE Common Weakness Enumeration entry for "Use of Hard-coded Credentials."

It is worth being precise about why `CWE-798` is treated as such a serious weakness class. The MITRE description captures two distinct failure modes. In one, the software contains an inbound authentication path with a credential that is the same across every deployment — a built-in account that the operator cannot change and may not even know exists. In the other, the software contains an outbound credential, embedded in the code or configuration, that it uses to authenticate to some other component. Both are dangerous, but for the same root reason: the secret is *static and shared*. It is identical across every unit of the same model, and it cannot be meaningfully rotated by the people operating the device.

That single property is what makes hardcoded credentials so corrosive. Normal credential hygiene assumes secrets are unique, changeable, and revocable. A hardcoded credential is none of those things. Compromise one device — or simply extract the credential by examining the firmware or configuration of any single unit — and you have not compromised one device. You have compromised the design. The same secret protects, or fails to protect, every other unit of that model in every facility that runs it. There is no rotation story, because rotation would require changing the software on every device. There is no "rotate and move on" incident response. The weakness is in the blueprint, not the instance.

Here is an illustrative, sanitized example of the *shape* of the problem — a configuration snippet of the kind that, in a poorly designed embedded device, embeds a credential directly. To be completely clear: this is a fabricated illustration to show the anti-pattern. It is not a real credential, not extracted from any device, and contains nothing usable.

```text title="illustrative-config-antipattern.xml" showLineNumbers
<!-- ILLUSTRATIVE ONLY. Not real. Demonstrates the CWE-798 anti-pattern. -->
<deviceConfig>
  <network>
    <managementEndpoint host="server.example.local" port="0000" />
  </network>
  <auth>
    <!-- The problem: a static credential shipped in config,
         identical across every unit, not operator-rotatable. -->
    <serviceAccount username="REDACTED_SERVICE_USER"
                    secret="REDACTED_STATIC_SECRET" />
  </auth>
</deviceConfig>
```

The thing to notice is not the specific values — they are deliberately meaningless. It is the *structure*. A secret living statically in a configuration file that ships with the device, used to authenticate the device to something else, is the textbook `CWE-798` pattern. The credential XML samples I produced for the research package were all of this sanitized, structural kind. The purpose was to document the weakness pattern for the threat model, never to provide anything operable.

Why does this anti-pattern persist in embedded and medical devices specifically? Because it is *convenient at manufacturing time*. A static credential means the device can talk to its management system out of the box with zero provisioning. There is no enrollment dance, no per-device key generation, no certificate lifecycle to manage on a constrained embedded platform. For a device designed years ago, under tighter compute and engineering constraints, a baked-in credential was the path of least resistance. The security cost of that convenience only becomes visible later, on a network the original designers never imagined.

## Building the threat model

With the DFD drawn and the two findings located on it, I built the threat model. I leaned on STRIDE as the structuring framework, because STRIDE maps cleanly onto a DFD — you walk each element and each data flow and ask which of the six threat categories apply: Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, and Elevation of Privilege.

The value of doing this systematically is that it stops you from fixating on the one bug you happened to find first. Walking the diagram element by element surfaces threats you would otherwise miss.

At the *network data flow* between the device and the management system, the dominant category is Information Disclosure — which is exactly where `CVE-2016-9355` lands. An observer on the shared segment can read data crossing the boundary. But STRIDE also forces you to consider Tampering on that same flow: if data can be read in transit, can it be modified in transit? And Spoofing: can an attacker impersonate the management server, or the device?

At the *authentication mechanism*, Spoofing and Elevation of Privilege dominate, and this is where `CWE-798` becomes acute. A hardcoded credential is a spoofing enabler by construction — if the secret is shared and extractable, an attacker who holds it can authenticate as a legitimate party. Worse, because the credential is identical across the fleet, a successful spoof against one unit generalizes. The threat model has to record this not as a localized risk but as a systemic one.

At the *configuration data store*, the categories are Information Disclosure (the credential can be read from the store) and Tampering (configuration can be altered if access controls are weak). At the *physical-access boundary*, you have to consider what an attacker with hands on the device can do — extract the configuration, read the credential, and from there pivot to the network-spoofing scenario above.

What the STRIDE pass made vivid is the *chaining*. None of these threats is most dangerous in isolation. The hardcoded credential (Spoofing/EoP) combined with the shared network position (Information Disclosure) combined with a flat network topology produces an attack path far more serious than any single finding suggests. That is the real output of a threat model: not a list of bugs, but a map of how they compose.

## Remediation and compensating controls

The honest difficulty with both findings is the one I opened with — you often cannot just fix the device. The hardcoded credential is in the design; rectifying it properly means a software change subject to the device's full validation and regulatory lifecycle. The data-exposure issue may be similarly constrained. So the practical security conversation for a device like this is less about patching and more about *compensating controls* deployed around it.

The single highest-value control is *network segmentation*. If a clinical device cannot be hardened, the network it lives on can be. Placing infusion pumps and other clinical equipment on isolated VLANs or dedicated segments, separated from general IT and tightly firewalled, directly attacks the positional risk that amplifies both findings. The data-exposure vulnerability matters far less when the set of parties who can occupy the network position to observe the traffic is reduced to a tiny, monitored population. The hardcoded-credential spoofing scenario matters far less when an attacker cannot reach the segment to use the credential in the first place. Segmentation does not fix the device, but it shrinks the attack surface dramatically.

The second control is *monitoring*. Clinical devices are notoriously opaque — you usually cannot install an endpoint agent on a regulated pump. But you can monitor the *network around* it. Because these devices have predictable, narrow communication patterns, anomaly detection at the network layer is unusually effective: a pump that suddenly talks to a host it has never contacted, or a spike in authentication attempts, stands out against an otherwise boring baseline. Passive network monitoring of clinical segments is one of the few telemetry sources available, and it is worth investing in.

The third is *access control and inventory*. Strict control over physical access to devices addresses the physical-extraction path for the credential. And — less glamorously but critically — an accurate inventory of which device models, at which firmware levels, are deployed where. You cannot manage the risk of a known-vulnerable device model if you do not know how many you have or where they are. A large fraction of medical-device risk is really an asset-management failure wearing a vulnerability costume.

The fourth is *procurement and lifecycle pressure*. The long-term fix is not technical at the hospital level; it is demanding better from the supply chain. The maturing FDA guidance on device cybersecurity, and the expectation of a software bill of materials and a defined support lifecycle, give purchasers leverage they did not have when the 8015-era devices were designed. The framing of medical-device security has shifted from "is the device safe?" to "is the device safe *and* secure across its whole supported life, on a real network?" That shift, applied at procurement, is what eventually drains the install base of devices that bake credentials into their configuration.

## Broader lessons for IoMT

Stepping back from the specific device, the two findings rhyme with the entire Internet of Medical Things, and the lessons generalize.

The first lesson is that *static secrets are a design-level liability, not an instance-level bug*. `CWE-798` is dangerous precisely because it scales with the install base. Any IoMT design that ships a shared, unrotatable credential is making a decision whose blast radius is the entire fleet. The remediation is architectural — unique per-device identity, provisioned at deployment, rotatable in the field — and it has to be designed in from the start, because retrofitting it into a fielded, regulated device is exactly the hard problem we have been circling.

The second lesson is that *the network is the real perimeter for devices that cannot defend themselves*. When you cannot patch the endpoint, the segment becomes your control plane. Both of my findings are far more dangerous on a flat network than a segmented one. For an entire class of devices that will remain un-patchable for years, network architecture is not a backstop — it is the primary defense.

The third lesson is that *threat modeling beats bug hunting* for this kind of asset. If I had gone looking for a single exploit, I would have found one CVE and stopped. Drawing the data flows, naming the trust boundaries, and walking STRIDE across them is what revealed the composition — the way a data-exposure issue and a hardcoded credential and a flat topology combine into something worse than their sum. For long-lived, hard-to-change devices, understanding the whole attack surface is more durable than chasing individual flaws, because the surface outlives any single patch.

The last lesson is the one I keep returning to. Medical devices do not stay vulnerable for years because anyone is negligent in a single, identifiable moment. They stay vulnerable because the lifecycle is long, the safety constraints are real, the patching pipeline is heavy, and the design assumptions were frozen in an earlier era of the internet. A hardcoded credential that was a reasonable manufacturing convenience in one decade becomes a fleet-wide weakness in the next, on a network nobody anticipated. Securing this space is less about finding the clever bug and more about reckoning, honestly, with that drift — and building the controls, the inventory discipline, and the procurement pressure to live with the devices we cannot simply replace.

Every year has a shape. By the time you reach the midpoint, you can already see the contour of the threats that will define it — which technologies are bleeding, which actors are ascendant, and which assumptions defenders held at the start of January have quietly collapsed by June.

The first half of 2026 has a remarkably clear shape, and it is not a comfortable one. Three storylines run through these six months and braid together by the time we reach the summer.

The first is the edge. Network edge devices — the VPN concentrators, SD-WAN controllers, and firewall management planes that sit at the perimeter and broker trust for everyone behind them — were the single most exploited class of target this half. Cisco alone disclosed and patched its sixth actively exploited SD-WAN zero-day of the year by mid-May. Check Point closed out the half with a critical VPN authentication flaw exploited weeks before its advisory. These are not incidental bugs in obscure software. They are pre-authentication breaks in the exact appliances organizations buy *to* enforce authentication.

The second storyline is extortion without encryption. The classic ransomware playbook — encrypt everything, demand payment for the key — kept eroding. Ransom payment rates fell, encryptionless data-leak extortion became the default, and a handful of professionalized affiliate programs consolidated the market. The criminals adapted faster than many of their victims did.

The third storyline is a single threat group's relentless run. ShinyHunters surfaced again and again across the half, claiming breach after breach against household-name brands — dating apps, retailers, education platforms, telecoms. When one actor's name attaches to that many incidents in six months, it stops being a series of events and becomes a pattern worth studying.

A note on method before we begin. This timeline is deliberately conservative. Every specific claim — every CVE identifier, severity score, date, victim, actor name, and statistic — traces to confirmed vendor advisories or reputable incident reporting, and I have cited each one inline. Where the public record is hedged, I hedge with it. The point of a timeline like this is not to be exhaustive; it is to be trustworthy, so that the patterns drawn from it rest on solid ground rather than on rumor or the inflated breach-count figures that circulate every quarter. I have left out incidents I could not anchor to a source, even where they were widely discussed.

What follows is a month-by-month account of the events that mattered, built only from confirmed reporting and vendor advisories. After the timeline, I pull back to examine the patterns — what these events say collectively about where attacker tradecraft is heading, and what defenders should be doing about it now.

## January

The year opened with a rare piece of good news from the law-enforcement side of the ledger, followed by a reminder that the criminal ecosystem is far larger than any single takedown.

### RAMP cybercrime forum seized

In January, authorities seized the RAMP cybercrime forum, one of the long-running Russian-language marketplaces where initial-access brokers, ransomware affiliates, and data sellers met to do business. Forum seizures matter less for the immediate disruption — communities scatter and reconstitute quickly — and more for the intelligence they yield: member lists, transaction histories, and the connective tissue between otherwise siloed criminal operations. The RAMP seizure is documented in Kaspersky's [State of Ransomware in 2026](https://securelist.com/state-of-ransomware-in-2026/119761/) retrospective, which treats it as one of several enforcement wins that nonetheless failed to slow the overall pace of extortion.

### ShinyHunters claims Match Group

Early in 2026, the threat actor ShinyHunters claimed a breach of Match Group — the parent company behind Tinder, Hinge, and OkCupid, as reported by [ACI Learning](https://www.acilearning.com/blog/the-biggest-cybersecurity-breaches-of-2026-so-far-and-the-training-that-could-have-prevented-them/). Dating platforms are an especially sensitive target because the data they hold is intimate by design: sexual orientation, relationship status, private messages, precise location history. A breach here is not merely a financial-fraud risk; it is a coercion and extortion risk against individual users. This was the first of what would become a long string of ShinyHunters claims across the half.

## February

If January hinted at the year's themes, February delivered the first hard proof of the edge-device story — and it arrived as a perfect-score zero-day.

### Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN: a CVSS 10.0 auth bypass

On February 25, 2026, Cisco disclosed `CVE-2026-20127`, an authentication bypass in the Catalyst SD-WAN Controller and Manager carrying a CVSS score of 10.0 — the maximum. According to [Tenable's analysis](https://www.tenable.com/blog/faq-about-the-continued-exploitation-of-cisco-catalyst-sd-wan-vulnerabilities-uat-8616), the flaw was already being exploited in the wild at the time of disclosure. An authentication bypass in an SD-WAN management plane is among the worst categories of vulnerability you can have: the controller orchestrates the entire software-defined network fabric, so breaking its authentication can hand an attacker control over routing, segmentation, and policy across every connected site.

To appreciate why this class of bug is so dangerous, it helps to picture what an SD-WAN controller actually does. In a software-defined WAN, the controller and its management layer are the brain of the network. They decide how traffic is routed between branch offices, data centers, and cloud, which paths are trusted, how segmentation is enforced, and what policy applies where. Every edge router in the fabric takes its marching orders from this plane. Break authentication on the brain, and you do not merely compromise one device — you potentially gain a lever over the routing and segmentation of the entire organization. That is why a maximum CVSS score on an SD-WAN management auth bypass is not hyperbole; it is an accurate reflection of how much trust the architecture places in a single component.

The same day, Cisco disclosed three companion vulnerabilities in the same family:

- `CVE-2026-20133` — CVSS 7.5, information disclosure
- `CVE-2026-20128` — CVSS 7.5, credential access
- `CVE-2026-20122` — CVSS 5.4, arbitrary file overwrite

None of these three was flagged as exploited on disclosure day. That would change. By April, all three were confirmed exploited in the wild, chained together by multiple threat clusters into a far more damaging sequence than any single bug suggested in isolation. The lesson here is one that recurs throughout 2026: a moderate-severity CVE does not stay moderate once it becomes a link in an exploitation chain.

## March

March was the month the SD-WAN problem went from a vendor advisory to an open free-for-all, and the month a hacktivist group reminded everyone that destruction, not extortion, is sometimes the point.

### A public PoC opens the floodgates

In early March, after a public proof-of-concept was released by ZeroZenX Labs, roughly ten additional threat clusters began exploiting the Cisco SD-WAN chain — `CVE-2026-20133`, `CVE-2026-20128`, and `CVE-2026-20122` together, per [Tenable](https://www.tenable.com/blog/faq-about-the-continued-exploitation-of-cisco-catalyst-sd-wan-vulnerabilities-uat-8616). This is the predictable, depressing arc of edge-device vulnerabilities. A flaw is disclosed; a small number of sophisticated actors exploit it quietly; a researcher publishes working exploit code; and within days the exploitation curve goes vertical as commodity attackers pile in. The window between "patch available" and "everyone is being scanned and hit" keeps shrinking.

### Handala wipes Stryker

In March, the Iran-linked hacktivist group Handala struck the medical-technology firm Stryker, wiping systems in real time, as reported by [CM-Alliance](https://www.cm-alliance.com/cybersecurity-blog/biggest-cyber-attacks-data-breaches-ransomware-attacks-of-march-2026) and [ACI Learning](https://www.acilearning.com/blog/the-biggest-cybersecurity-breaches-of-2026-so-far-and-the-training-that-could-have-prevented-them/). Wiper attacks are categorically different from ransomware. There is no decryption key on offer, no negotiation, no business transaction — only destruction, often for geopolitical signaling. When the target is a medical-device manufacturer, the downstream stakes extend to patient care and device supply chains. Hacktivist wipers against critical-adjacent industries were a recurring undertone of the half.

### LeakBase forum seized

March also brought a second forum takedown: LeakBase was seized, again documented in Kaspersky's [State of Ransomware in 2026](https://securelist.com/state-of-ransomware-in-2026/119761/). Two major forum seizures in three months reflects genuine enforcement pressure on the data-leak economy — and yet, as the rest of this timeline shows, the volume of breaches and extortion only accelerated. Enforcement against the marketplaces does not, on its own, close off the supply of stolen data.

## April

April was a heavy month for ransomware against large institutions, and it brought a reported authentication-bypass affecting one of the most widely deployed control panels on the internet.

### A reported cPanel/WHM auth bypass

As reported by [ACI Learning](https://www.acilearning.com/blog/the-biggest-cybersecurity-breaches-of-2026-so-far-and-the-training-that-could-have-prevented-them/), a critical cPanel/WHM authentication bypass tracked as `CVE-2026-41940` came to light in April, with reporting indicating it had reportedly been exploited in the wild since around February. I phrase this carefully and as reported, because cPanel/WHM sits underneath an enormous share of shared-hosting and small-business web infrastructure. If an authentication bypass there was genuinely exploited for months before wider awareness, the population of potentially affected sites is vast — and it fits the half's broader theme of pre-auth flaws in trust-brokering software being exploited well ahead of public disclosure.

### Ransomware hits major institutions

[CM-Alliance's April roundup](https://www.cm-alliance.com/cybersecurity-blog/major-cyber-attacks-data-breaches-ransomware-attacks-in-april-2026) lists a striking set of ransomware targets for the month: the EU Commission, Booking.com, McGraw Hill, and Medtronic. The spread is instructive. A supranational government body, a global travel-booking platform, a major educational publisher, and a medical-device manufacturer have almost nothing in common operationally — which is precisely the point. Modern ransomware affiliates are opportunistic and sector-agnostic; they follow exposure and exploitability, not industry. A medical-device firm like Medtronic appearing alongside a travel platform underscores that no sector buys its way out of the target list.

## May

May was the busiest and most consequential month of the half by a wide margin — a sixth Cisco SD-WAN zero-day serious enough to trigger a federal emergency directive, an extraordinary cadence of large breaches, and a cluster of incidents touching AI infrastructure itself.

### The sixth SD-WAN zero-day and CISA Emergency Directive 26-03

On May 14, 2026, Cisco disclosed `CVE-2026-20182`, another CVSS 10.0 authentication bypass in Catalyst SD-WAN, active as a zero-day. Per [Tenable](https://www.tenable.com/blog/faq-about-the-continued-exploitation-of-cisco-catalyst-sd-wan-vulnerabilities-uat-8616) and [SecurityWeek](https://www.securityweek.com/cisco-patches-another-sd-wan-zero-day-the-sixth-exploited-in-2026/), this was the sixth SD-WAN flaw exploited in 2026 — six actively exploited zero-days in a single product family before the year was half over.

The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency responded the same day with Emergency Directive 26-03, setting a remediation deadline of May 17. A three-day window from directive to deadline is about as urgent as CISA gets, and it reflects the severity of an unauthenticated break in federal SD-WAN management planes.

### UAT-8616

Tenable attributes much of the sustained Cisco SD-WAN exploitation to a threat actor it tracks as UAT-8616, [active since roughly 2023](https://www.tenable.com/blog/faq-about-the-continued-exploitation-of-cisco-catalyst-sd-wan-vulnerabilities-uat-8616). The group targets critical infrastructure and shows a mature, methodical tradecraft: it uses the older `CVE-2022-20775` to obtain root, injects SSH keys for durable access, and clears logs to frustrate incident response. The combination of fresh zero-days for initial access and a years-old CVE for privilege escalation is a hallmark of a patient, well-resourced operator that treats edge appliances as long-term footholds rather than smash-and-grab targets.

### A relentless breach wave

[CM-Alliance's May roundup](https://www.cm-alliance.com/cybersecurity-blog/biggest-cyber-attacks-data-breaches-ransomware-attacks-of-may-2026) reads like a breach a day. The most significant, in chronological order:

- **May 3 — Instructure / Canvas**, attributed to ShinyHunters. [ACI Learning](https://www.acilearning.com/blog/the-biggest-cybersecurity-breaches-of-2026-so-far-and-the-training-that-could-have-prevented-them/) describes this as the largest education-sector breach. Canvas is a learning-management platform used by enormous numbers of students and institutions, so the blast radius of student and educator data is wide.
- **May 5 — Vimeo**, attributed to ShinyHunters, affecting 119,000 users.
- **May 8 — Zara / Inditex**, affecting 197,000 customers.
- **May 18 — NYC Health + Hospitals**, exposing 1.8 million medical records along with biometrics. Health records plus biometrics is a worst-case combination: biometric identifiers cannot be rotated the way a password can.
- **May 19 — 7-Eleven**, attributed to ShinyHunters.
- **May 26 — Charter Communications**, a ShinyHunters extortion threat over 5 million customers.
- **May 28 — Carnival Corporation**, affecting 6 million people.

ShinyHunters' name attaches to four of these seven incidents in a single month. That is not coincidence; it is the signature of an operation running at industrial scale against a pipeline of pre-identified targets.

### Ransomware in May

The same [CM-Alliance May roundup](https://www.cm-alliance.com/cybersecurity-blog/biggest-cyber-attacks-data-breaches-ransomware-attacks-of-may-2026) tracks a parallel wave of ransomware:

- **May 4 — Mediaworks**, hit by World Leaks with 8.5 TB exfiltrated.
- **May 12 — Foxconn**, hit by Nitrogen, with stolen files reportedly relating to Apple and NVIDIA — a supply-chain concern given Foxconn's role as a contract manufacturer.
- **May 15 — West Pharmaceutical**, encrypted, with manufacturing disrupted. This is the dangerous case where extortion crosses into physical-world impact: a pharmaceutical manufacturing halt has real consequences for drug supply.
- **May 18 — Grafana Labs**, where the actor TeamPCP stole source code; Grafana Labs refused to pay the ransom.

### When the attack surface is AI itself

Three May incidents stand out because they involve AI infrastructure on both the victim and attacker side:

- **May 1 — Canonical / Ubuntu** was hit with a DDoS attack by the Iraq-linked "313 Team."
- **May 14 — OpenAI** suffered a supply-chain compromise of two employee devices, attributed to TeamPCP. A compromise of employee endpoints at a frontier AI lab is exactly the kind of supply-chain foothold that warrants close attention, given the sensitivity of model and infrastructure access.
- **May 28 — GreyVibe** conducted automated attacks using ChatGPT and Gemini. This is the leading edge of attacker tooling: large language models driving automation in the offensive loop, not just assisting human operators.

All three are documented in [CM-Alliance's May roundup](https://www.cm-alliance.com/cybersecurity-blog/biggest-cyber-attacks-data-breaches-ransomware-attacks-of-may-2026).

## June

June, as of this writing on the 14th, is defined by one event that ties the half's dominant theme together with a bow: another edge appliance, another pre-authentication break, exploited well before the advisory.

### Check Point VPN: CVE-2026-50751

On June 8, 2026, Check Point published an advisory for `CVE-2026-50751`, a CVSS 9.3 improper-authentication flaw — specifically a certificate-validation logic error — in its Remote Access VPN, Mobile Access, and Spark Firewall products, spanning versions R80.20.X through R82.10. Per [Rapid7's analysis](https://www.rapid7.com/blog/post/etr-critical-check-point-vpn-zero-day-exploited-in-the-wild-cve-2026-50751/), the flaw lets an unauthenticated attacker establish a VPN session without credentials.

Two details make this worse than a routine critical CVE. First, it had been exploited in the wild since May 7, 2026 — roughly a month before the advisory — with activity increasing in early June. Second, the impact is direct and total for the affected function: a VPN whose entire job is to verify identity before granting network access can be made to grant that access to anyone. CISA added it to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on June 9, 2026, the day after disclosure.

Rapid7 also notes a related flaw, `CVE-2026-50752` (CVSS 7.4), for which no exploitation has been observed.

## The patterns

Step back from the day-by-day, and four patterns dominate the first half of 2026.

### The perimeter is the breach

The edge devices organizations deploy to enforce trust have become the primary mechanism for breaking it. Consider the year's marquee CVEs.

| CVE | Disclosed | CVSS | Product | Class | Exploited |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `CVE-2026-20127` | Feb 25 | 10.0 | Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Controller/Manager | Auth bypass | At disclosure |
| `CVE-2026-20133` | Feb 25 | 7.5 | Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN | Info disclosure | By April |
| `CVE-2026-20128` | Feb 25 | 7.5 | Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN | Credential access | By April |
| `CVE-2026-20122` | Feb 25 | 5.4 | Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN | Arbitrary file overwrite | By April |
| `CVE-2026-41940` | April (reported) | — | cPanel / WHM | Auth bypass | Reportedly since ~Feb |
| `CVE-2026-20182` | May 14 | 10.0 | Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN | Auth bypass | At disclosure (zero-day) |
| `CVE-2026-50751` | June 8 | 9.3 | Check Point Remote Access VPN / Mobile Access / Spark | Improper authentication | Since May 7 |

The throughline is unmistakable. The two perfect-10.0 scores and the 9.3 are all authentication breaks in appliances whose sole purpose is authentication. Two of the most serious were exploited as zero-days — live before any patch existed. And in the Check Point and cPanel cases, exploitation preceded the advisory by weeks or months, which means defenders who patched promptly on disclosure day were already, potentially, too late.

Why this class of device, and why now? Part of the answer is structural. Edge appliances are internet-facing by definition — that is their job — so they are continuously reachable and continuously scanned. They run vendor firmware that customers cannot easily inspect or instrument, which means the same endpoint-detection tooling defenders rely on across their laptop and server fleets often has no visibility into the appliance itself. And they are operationally awkward to patch: taking a VPN concentrator or SD-WAN controller offline disrupts everyone behind it, so patches get deferred. The combination — always reachable, poorly observable, slow to patch — makes edge devices an attacker's ideal target. The 2026 record simply confirms that attackers have internalized this and are prioritizing accordingly.

There is also a structural lesson in the February Cisco cluster. Three of the four flaws disclosed that day were rated 7.5 or lower and were *not* flagged as exploited at disclosure. By April, all three were confirmed exploited, chained together. CVSS severity scored in isolation badly underestimates risk when bugs combine. Treat "moderate" edge-device CVEs in trust-brokering software as latent links in a future chain, not as items you can safely defer.

### Exploitation outruns disclosure, and PoCs detonate

The March Cisco episode is the cleanest illustration of a dynamic that defined the half: the moment ZeroZenX Labs released a public proof-of-concept, roughly ten additional threat clusters jumped onto the SD-WAN chain. The interval between exploit-code availability and mass exploitation is now measured in days. Combined with the Check Point and cPanel cases — where exploitation ran for weeks or months ahead of the advisory — the practical conclusion is that the patch-on-disclosure model is necessary but no longer sufficient. Compensating controls, exposure reduction, and assume-breach monitoring on edge devices have to carry weight that patching alone cannot.

### Ransomware grew up, and stopped relying on encryption

Kaspersky's [State of Ransomware in 2026](https://securelist.com/state-of-ransomware-in-2026/119761/) documents a maturing, professionalizing criminal market, and the shifts are worth naming individually.

The ransom payment rate fell to 28% in 2025. As fewer victims pay for decryption, the economics push attackers toward encryptionless, data-leak extortion — steal the data, threaten to publish it, skip the disruptive and increasingly futile encryption step. The May breach wave, dominated by exfiltration-and-extortion rather than lock-up-and-demand, reflects exactly this.

The tradecraft hardened in parallel. The PE32 ransomware family adopted ML-KEM (Kyber1024) post-quantum encryption — criminals adopting post-quantum cryptography to protect *their* operations against future decryption. Bring-your-own-vulnerable-driver "EDR killers" became standard tooling for disabling endpoint defenses before acting. And RDWeb portals replaced exposed RDP as the preferred initial-access vector, a shift defenders monitoring only for classic RDP exposure may miss.

The market also consolidated around a few dominant operations: Qilin, a ransomware-as-a-service program running since Q2 2025; Clop, with its supply-chain focus; Akira; and an emerging group called "The Gentlemen." Consolidation around professionalized affiliate programs means more consistent tradecraft and higher operational tempo — exactly what the breach calendar showed.

Each of these shifts has a defensive implication worth stating plainly. If payment rates are falling and extortion is moving to pure data theft, then a clean, tested backup — long the canonical ransomware countermeasure — no longer protects you from the harm; it protects you from downtime, but not from publication. The defensive center of gravity has to move upstream, toward preventing exfiltration in the first place and detecting large or anomalous outbound data flows. If "EDR killers" using vulnerable drivers are standard, then driver allow-listing and tamper-protection on endpoint agents stop being nice-to-haves. And if RDWeb portals have displaced exposed RDP as the favored entry point, then the monitoring playbooks written for classic RDP exposure need updating, because they will quietly miss the new front door.

### AI is now on both sides of the line

The May cluster — the OpenAI employee-device supply-chain compromise, and GreyVibe's automated attacks driving ChatGPT and Gemini — marks AI infrastructure as both a high-value target and an active offensive instrument, per [CM-Alliance](https://www.cm-alliance.com/cybersecurity-blog/biggest-cyber-attacks-data-breaches-ransomware-attacks-of-may-2026). Attackers compromising a frontier lab's endpoints and attackers using frontier models to automate their campaigns are two faces of the same shift. This is early, but the trajectory is clear, and it deserves dedicated attention in the second half of the year.

### What to do about it

If I compress the half into actionable guidance: treat your edge appliances — VPNs, SD-WAN controllers, firewall and hosting control planes — as the most likely point of initial compromise, not the most defended. Patch them fastest, monitor them hardest, and assume that disclosure-day patching may already be behind the attackers. Reduce their exposure to the internet wherever the architecture allows. Watch for credential-access and SSH-key-injection persistence, not just the initial exploit. And rebalance ransomware defenses toward preventing and detecting exfiltration, because the encryption step — the part legacy defenses were built to catch — is increasingly optional for the people trying to extort you.

The shape of 2026 is the shape of trust being broken at the perimeter. The second half will tell us whether defenders adjusted in time.

"Zero trust" gets thrown around as a product you can buy. It is not. It is an architectural approach, defined rigorously in **NIST SP 800-207**, where access decisions are made per request based on identity and context rather than network location.

## The core principles

NIST frames zero trust around a simple shift: stop trusting the network. The guiding ideas are:

- **Never trust, always verify.** No request is trusted because it came from inside the perimeter.
- **Least privilege.** Grant the minimum access needed, for the minimum time.
- **Assume breach.** Design as if an attacker is already inside, and limit how far they can move.

The classic castle-and-moat model fails because once an attacker is past the firewall, they roam freely. Zero trust removes the implicit trust of "inside."

## The identity-centric perimeter

In SP 800-207 the heart of the system is the **Policy Decision Point (PDP)** and **Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)**. Every access attempt flows through a PEP, which asks the PDP whether to allow it. The decision draws on:

- The authenticated identity of the user or workload.
- Device posture and health.
- Resource sensitivity and request context.

The perimeter becomes identity, not IP range. Strong authentication (MFA, phishing-resistant credentials) is the foundation.

## Microsegmentation and mTLS

Assume-breach means limiting lateral movement. **Microsegmentation** divides the network into small zones so a compromised workload cannot reach everything. Between services, **mutual TLS (mTLS)** ensures both sides prove identity with certificates, not just the client trusting the server.

A service mesh can enforce this. Here is a minimal policy that requires mTLS and restricts who may call a service:

```yaml title="payments-policy.yaml" showLineNumbers {6,12}
apiVersion: security.istio.io/v1
kind: PeerAuthentication
metadata:
  name: payments-mtls
spec:
  mtls:
    mode: STRICT          # reject any non-mTLS traffic
---
apiVersion: security.istio.io/v1
kind: AuthorizationPolicy
metadata:
  name: payments-allow
spec:
  selector:
    matchLabels:
      app: payments
  rules:
    - from:
        - source:
            principals: ["cluster.local/ns/checkout/sa/checkout"]
```

This says: only the `checkout` service account may call `payments`, and only over verified mTLS. Every other request is denied by default.

## A practical blueprint

You do not rebuild everything at once. A pragmatic path:

1. **Inventory** identities, devices, and the resources worth protecting.
2. **Centralize identity** behind a single strong IdP with MFA.
3. **Put a PEP in front of sensitive resources** (an identity-aware proxy or gateway).
4. **Segment** the highest-value workloads first, then expand.
5. **Enforce mTLS** between services as you migrate to a mesh.
6. **Log every decision** so the PDP's choices are auditable.

> Tip: Start with one critical application end to end rather than trying to "zero-trust" the whole network. A complete vertical slice teaches you more than a broad, shallow rollout.

## The payoff

Done well, zero trust shrinks the blast radius of any single compromise. A stolen credential no longer means free movement across the environment, because each hop is independently verified against policy. That is the difference between a buzzword and a blueprint.

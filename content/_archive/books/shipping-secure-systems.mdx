## About the book

Security is not a feature you bolt on at the end of a sprint. It is a set of habits, defaults, and trade-offs that you live with from the first commit to the final deploy. *Shipping Secure Systems* is written for the engineers who actually build and operate software, not for the compliance binder that nobody reads.

This book treats threat modeling as a design tool rather than a ceremony. It walks through how attackers think, where real-world breaches actually begin, and why the most expensive incidents almost always trace back to a boring, preventable mistake. The goal is not paranoia. The goal is calibrated judgment: knowing which risks deserve your attention this week and which ones can wait.

Every chapter is grounded in production realities. You will find decisions about secrets management, authentication flows, and dependency hygiene framed the way they appear on the job, with deadlines, legacy code, and imperfect information.

## What's inside

- Threat modeling that fits in a one-hour meeting and still finds real problems
- Designing secure defaults so the easy path is also the safe path
- Authentication and session handling without the common footguns
- Managing secrets, keys, and credentials across environments
- Dependency and supply-chain risk, and how to keep it from quietly growing
- Logging and monitoring that help you during an incident instead of after it
- A pragmatic incident response playbook for small and mid-sized teams

## Who it's for

This book is for software engineers, tech leads, and platform teams who own systems in production and want to raise their security baseline without slowing to a crawl. If you have ever wondered whether your service is one bad input away from a headline, this is for you.

You do not need a background in cryptography or a security certification. You need working knowledge of how modern web services are built and a willingness to question your defaults. Readers comfortable with a terminal and a code review will get the most from the worked examples, but the principles apply regardless of your stack.

By the end, you should be able to look at a system, sketch its threat model, and ship changes that make it measurably harder to breach.
